Sub-Saharan Africa’s population is expected to increase by 124% in the next 20 years, yet its grid extension has advanced slower than in any other major regions in the world; approximately one third of the urban residents and 8% of the rural population are connected to the electrical grid (Rabah 2005). Not only is grid connection sparse across the region, but problems of intermittency and reliability are increasingly evident (Rabah 2005). While Sub-Saharan Africa is the poorest global region in terms of energy access, it is the richest in solar endowment (Quansah 2016); as depicted in figure 3, most african nations receive more than 2000 kWh of global solar radiation annually.

In addition to lowest global energy access rates, Sub-Saharan Africa contains the highest food insecurity rates, lowest literacy and primary school rates, and the lowest health outcomes globally (i.e. infant and maternal mortality rates etc) (Amankwah-Amoah). Due to high solar radiation levels coupled with high levels of impoverishment, Sub-Saharan Africa has not only been a primary location for foreign aid and development programs, but additionally has seen an increase in foreign and state led solar PV initiatives. Small-scale decentralized solar systems have particularly been chosen and portrayed as the ‘cure-all’ to the region’s impoverishment.
